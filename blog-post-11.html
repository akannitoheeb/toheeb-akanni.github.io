Email Strategy

What Is Audience Segmentation and Why It Triples Your Email Results

TA
Toheeb Akanni July 2026
⏱ 11 min read

Last quarter, I was working with a skincare brand in Lagos. They had 3,200 subscribers on their email list and were sending the same newsletter to everyone — new buyers, repeat customers, people who had not opened an email in six months. Their open rate was 11%. Their click rate was 1.2%. They were about to quit email marketing entirely.

I asked them one question: "When you walk into a store in Lagos, does the seller greet a first-time visitor the same way they greet a customer who has bought ten times before?" They looked at me like I was stating the obvious. "Of course not."

"Then why," I asked, "are you sending the exact same email to both?"

We segmented their list into five groups. We rewrote their emails for each group. Within 30 days, their open rate jumped to 34%. Their click rate hit 6.8%. And their monthly revenue from email alone tripled. That is the power of audience segmentation. It is not magic. It is respect.

"Sending the same message to everyone on your list is like walking into a room of 1,000 people and shouting one sentence, hoping it resonates with all of them. Segmentation is walking up to each person and speaking directly to what they care about."

What Is Audience Segmentation, Really?

Audience segmentation is the practice of dividing your email list into smaller groups based on shared characteristics, behaviours, or interests. Instead of broadcasting one message to your entire list, you send targeted messages to specific segments.

Think of it this way. Your email list is not one audience. It is a collection of many audiences. A subscriber who bought from you yesterday has a completely different relationship with your brand than someone who subscribed six months ago and never opened an email. A customer in Lagos who buys every month is not the same as a one-time buyer in Abuja who only purchased because of a discount.

When you treat them as the same person, you fail both of them. The loyal customer gets bored by beginner content. The new subscriber gets overwhelmed by advanced offers. Neither engages. Both unsubscribe.

When you segment, you speak to each person as who they actually are. The loyal customer gets early access to new products. The new subscriber gets a welcome series that builds trust. The inactive subscriber gets a re-engagement campaign. Everyone gets what they need. Everyone engages more.

  • 9.37% lower unsubscribe rates than non-segmented campaigns

The Five Segments Every Nigerian Business Needs

You do not need complex algorithms or expensive software to start segmenting. Here are the five segments I create for almost every client. They are simple to implement and immediately effective.

Segment 1

New Subscribers (0–30 Days)

These people just joined your list. They do not know you yet. They are curious but cautious. Your goal is not to sell. Your goal is to build trust.

What to send: A welcome series that introduces your brand story, shares your values, delivers immediate value (a tip, guide, or discount), and sets expectations for future emails.

What not to send: Aggressive sales pitches. They do not know if they can trust you yet. Pushing for a sale now feels desperate and trains them to ignore future emails.

Example: A Lagos-based fitness coach sends new subscribers a free 7-day meal plan in the welcome series. No sales pitch. Just value. By day 5, subscribers are replying to emails with questions. By day 7, they are ready to hear about the paid programme.

Segment 2

Active Buyers (Purchased in Last 90 Days)

These are your most valuable subscribers. They have proven they will spend money with you. Your goal is to deepen the relationship and increase purchase frequency.

What to send: Product recommendations based on their purchase history, early access to new arrivals, loyalty rewards, and upsell opportunities for complementary products.

What not to send: Introductory content about who you are. They already know. Treat them like insiders, not prospects.

Example: A shoe store in Lagos segments buyers by category. Someone who bought running shoes gets emails about athletic socks, insoles, and new running shoe releases. Someone who bought formal shoes gets content about leather care and new formal collections. Both feel understood. Both buy again.

Segment 3

Lapsed Customers (No Purchase in 90–180 Days)

These customers bought before but have gone quiet. They are not lost yet, but they are drifting. Your goal is to remind them why they bought from you in the first place.

What to send: "We miss you" campaigns with a personalised incentive, new product announcements that align with their previous purchase, and social proof showing what other customers are buying now.

What not to send: Generic newsletters that assume they are still engaged. They are not. You need to re-earn their attention.

Example: A beauty brand sends lapsed customers an email with the subject line: "[First Name], your favourite serum has a new formula." It references their specific past purchase, shows the improved version, and offers a 15% return-customer discount. The personalisation and incentive combine to win them back.

Segment 4

High Engagers (Open 50%+ of Emails, Never Bought)

This is the most misunderstood segment. They read every email. They click links. They visit your website. But they have never purchased. They are interested but something is holding them back.

What to send: Content that removes purchase barriers — testimonials, case studies, FAQ emails addressing common objections, limited-time offers with clear value propositions, and social proof showing results.

What not to send: More of the same content they are already consuming. They do not need more information. They need a reason to act.

Example: A business consultant in Lagos has 200 subscribers who open every email but never book a call. She sends a targeted email with the subject: "The #1 reason smart business owners hesitate to hire a consultant (and why it costs them)." The email addresses the objection directly, shares a client transformation story, and offers a free 15-minute consultation with no commitment. She books 12 calls from that one email.

Segment 5

Inactive Subscribers (No Opens in 90+ Days)

These subscribers have gone cold. They are hurting your deliverability and dragging down your metrics. But before you delete them, give them one last chance to re-engage.

What to send: A re-engagement campaign with a direct subject line like "Should I keep you on this list?" Offer a compelling reason to stay — exclusive content, a special discount, or a simple preference update where they choose what they want to receive.

What not to send: More of your regular content. They are already ignoring it. You need a pattern interrupt.

Example: A fashion brand sends inactive subscribers an email with the subject: "Last call: do you still want to hear from us?" The body is honest and brief: "We noticed you have not opened our emails in a while. No hard feelings — inboxes are crowded. But if you still want style tips and early access to sales, click here. If not, we will remove you and wish you well." The transparency and respect earn replies. Some re-engage. Others unsubscribe cleanly. Both outcomes improve the list quality.

How to Segment Your List in Brevo (For Free)

If you use Brevo — which I recommend for Nigerian businesses because of its generous free tier — segmentation is built in and easy to set up. Here is how:

Step 1: Create Segments Based on Contact Properties

  • Go to Contacts → Segments → Create a Segment
  • Name it clearly: "Active Buyers — Last 90 Days" or "New Subscribers — 0-30 Days"
  • Set conditions: "Date of last purchase is less than 90 days ago" or "Date added to list is less than 30 days ago"

Step 2: Create Segments Based on Email Engagement

  • "High Engagers" = opened at least 5 of last 10 emails AND never purchased
  • "Inactive" = has not opened any email in last 90 days
  • "Lapsed" = last purchase between 90 and 180 days ago

Step 3: Tag Contacts Based on Behaviour

  • Use automation workflows to add tags when contacts take actions
  • "Purchased — Running Shoes" tag added after that purchase
  • "Clicked — Pricing Page" tag added after that click
  • "Downloaded — Lead Magnet" tag added after form submission

Step 4: Send Targeted Campaigns to Each Segment

  • When creating a campaign, select the segment instead of your entire list
  • Write the subject line and body specifically for that segment's mindset
  • Track results separately to see which segments respond best

All of this is free on Brevo's free plan. You do not need to upgrade to start segmenting effectively.

Segmentation Mistakes That Kill Results

I have seen businesses try segmentation and fail. Here is why:

  • Over-segmenting too early — creating 20 segments when you have 500 subscribers. Start with 3–5 segments. Expand as your list grows.
  • Segments with no strategy — creating a "Lagos subscribers" segment but sending them the same content as everyone else. If the segment does not change what you send, it is useless.
  • Never updating segments — a subscriber who was "new" six months ago is not new anymore. Segments must be dynamic and update automatically based on behaviour.
  • Ignoring the unsegmented — some subscribers will not fit neatly into any segment. Create a "General" segment for them rather than blasting your entire list.
  • Segmenting by demographics alone — age and location matter less than behaviour. A 25-year-old in Lagos who buys monthly is more similar to a 45-year-old in Abuja who buys monthly than to another 25-year-old in Lagos who has never purchased.

The Segmentation Strategy I Use for Every New Client

When I take on a new email marketing client, this is the exact process I follow:

Week 1: Audit and Tag

  • Export the full contact list
  • Identify available data: purchase history, email engagement, signup source, location, preferences
  • Create basic segments: New, Active, Lapsed, Inactive, High Engagers
  • Set up automation rules to tag new contacts automatically going forward

Week 2: Write Segment-Specific Content

  • Welcome series for New subscribers
  • Product recommendations and loyalty content for Active buyers
  • Win-back campaign for Lapsed customers
  • Re-engagement sequence for Inactive subscribers
  • Objection-busting content for High Engagers who have not bought

Week 3: Launch and Measure

  • Send the first segmented campaigns
  • Compare open rates, click rates, and revenue per segment
  • Identify which segments are most responsive and double down
  • Adjust messaging for underperforming segments

Week 4: Refine and Expand

  • Add behavioural tags based on email clicks and website visits
  • Create sub-segments within high-performing groups
  • Test subject lines and send times per segment
  • Document what works for each segment and build a playbook

By the end of month one, every client sees measurable improvement. By month three, the results compound. Segmentation is not a one-time task. It is a continuous refinement process that gets better the more you do it.

✅ Your email list is not a monolith. It is a mosaic. Every subscriber is at a different stage of their relationship with your brand. Audience segmentation is how you honour that difference. It is how you turn a broadcast into a conversation. And it is how you triple your email results without adding a single new subscriber.

Your Next Step

If you are sending the same email to your entire list right now, you are leaving money on the table. Not because your emails are bad. Because they are not relevant to everyone receiving them.

Start today. Log into your email platform. Create three segments: New Subscribers, Active Buyers, and Inactive Subscribers. Write one targeted email for each. Send them. Measure the difference. I guarantee your open rates will jump. Your click rates will jump. And your revenue from email will follow.

Segmentation is not advanced email marketing. It is foundational email marketing. The brands that master it win. The brands that ignore it wonder why email "does not work" for them.
